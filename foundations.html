01 · Mindset

The Apprentice Mindset

You’ve shipped real code, fixed real bugs, survived a few production incidents. That matters. What’s next is a different move — not “learn more syntax”, but grow the two muscles that turn a coder into a reliable teammate: curiosity and commitment.

Why “apprentice” is the right stance

Some people frame themselves by title or by how long they’ve been on the team. The apprentice stance is different: you are here to learn, to ask, to try, to miss, and to learn again. Someone who waits to be told, stays told-sized. An apprentice watches, asks, and practises. The title doesn’t change — the stance does. That’s what this guide is about.

Key Insight

Time at the keyboard and time practising are not the same thing. Ten engineers can have the same amount of experience on paper and half of them will have one year repeated many times. The difference is whether you grew the habits, or just the timeline.

The two muscles

Curiosity

You question. You dig into why things work. You notice anti-patterns. You ask, “is there a better way?” You read the source. You explore. You poke at the framework you use every day until you actually understand it.

Commitment

You finish what you start. Your yes means yes. You deliver what you said by when you said. You don’t take on five things and finish one. You keep small promises reliably so people trust you with bigger ones.

💡

Why these two, specifically? Because they are the preconditions for everything else. You cannot write clean code without the curiosity to ask, “is this the right name?” You cannot deliver reliably without the commitment to see work through. Every other chapter in this guide is a consequence of these two.

What the shift looks like day-to-day

SituationTime servedTime practised
Given a storyStart codingRead it twice; ask “what’s the business reason?” if it’s not obvious
Stuck on a bugAsk a teammate after 2 minutesTry for 15–30 minutes, write down what you tried, then ask
Seeing an anti-patternCopy it; “that’s how it’s done here”Ask “why?”; if the answer is weak, propose an alternative
In a huddleListen quietlyAsk at least one question or offer one observation
Given feedbackExplain why you did it that waySay thanks, try it, come back with what you learned
Finished a storyMove onRun it yourself, check the edge cases, read your own MR top to bottom
Saw a slow test / flaky buildIgnore — “not my story”Mention it; if you can fix it in 30 minutes, fix it

Questions to sit with

A weekly mirror

  • What did I ask this week that I wouldn’t have asked six months ago?
  • What did I finish this week that I said I would?
  • What did I not finish, and what’s the honest reason?
  • Where did I copy a pattern without understanding it?
  • What was too hard this week that I should have asked for help on earlier?
02 · Muscle One

Curiosity as a Practice

Curiosity is not a personality trait; it’s a practice. You can build it the same way you build unit-test writing or git hygiene — by doing a small version of it every day until it’s automatic.

What curiosity actually looks like at work

It looks boring from the outside. Most of curiosity is the quiet, stubborn refusal to let something you don’t understand slide past.

The Curiosity Moves

  1. Pause on magic. When code “just works” and you don’t know why, that’s the signal. Stop. Read. Trace. Ask.
  2. Follow the call. When you use a library function, open the source at least once. You don’t have to read it all — ten minutes is enough.
  3. Ask a ‘why’ in every huddle. One real why. “Why this approach and not X?” “Why are we solving this now?”
  4. Write down what you don’t know. A running list. The list is your curriculum.
  5. Poke the framework. Read one chapter of the docs for something you use every day. You’ll find something that surprises you within a week.

A scenario: the magic annotation

🔎

You’re adding a new Spring controller. You copy the one next to yours. It has @Transactional at the top. Your new one needs it too — you copy it. Things work. Two months later a teammate asks, “why is this whole controller transactional, including the read-only endpoints?” You don’t know.

The curious move: the first time you see @Transactional, spend ten minutes. What does it actually do? Why is it on the class instead of the method? What happens if you call a non-annotated method from an annotated one? You don’t need to become a Spring expert — you need to not be surprised by your own code.

Patterns & anti-patterns

Common pitfall — Copy-paste without understanding

You find code that “looks like what I need.” You copy it, change the names, hope. It works. You never find out why.

Better — Copy-paste with a question

Copy it, change the names, then spend ten minutes understanding what each piece does. Delete what you don’t need. If you can’t delete it confidently, you don’t yet understand it.

Common pitfall — “That’s just how it’s done here”

You notice a pattern that looks odd. You assume someone smarter had a reason. You adopt it. The odd pattern spreads.

Better — “I noticed X. What’s the reason?”

Ask a peer or a senior. Half the time there’s a good reason (now you know it). Half the time the answer is “honestly, we should clean that up” — and now you’ve surfaced a real thing.

Common pitfall — Pretending to understand

Someone explains something in standup. You nod. You didn’t follow. You walk away still not knowing, and now you can’t ask without admitting you nodded.

Better — “Sorry, can you say that again?”

Asked in the first 30 seconds, it’s just a clarifying question. Asked a week later, it’s an admission. The cost of the first is zero; the cost of the second is your credibility.

Dos & Don’ts

DoDon’t
Keep a “things I don’t understand” listKeep faking it and hoping it goes away
Ask the “stupid” question in the first week, not the tenthSave clarification for later — it won’t come
Open the library’s source at least once a weekTreat dependencies as sealed black boxes
Poke at the thing that just worked mysteriouslySay “it works, don’t touch it” and move on
Ask “why this, not that?” in design discussionsAccept the first approach offered because a senior proposed it
🎯

Why this matters: Every senior engineer you admire got there by being the person who wouldn’t let an unknown slide. The gap between them and a “solid contributor” is not raw talent. It’s the number of times they went one level deeper when they could have moved on.

Further reading

  • The Pragmatic Programmer — Hunt & Thomas. The foundational book on the curious stance.
  • A Philosophy of Software Design — John Ousterhout. Why understanding beats pattern-matching.
  • Julia Evans’ blog (jvns.ca) — a masterclass in publicly practising curiosity.
03 · Muscle Two

Commitment as a Practice

Commitment is the quiet half of reliability. It’s not heroics. It’s the slow, boring, compound fact that your yes means yes. When people trust your yes, they give you more interesting work. When they don’t, they stop asking.

What commitment actually means

Commitment is not the same as availability, enthusiasm, or “wanting to help.” Those are cheap. Commitment is:

  1. You said you would do X by Y.
  2. You did X by Y.
  3. Or: you knew by Y-2-days that you wouldn’t, and you said so in time for people to adjust.

That’s it. The whole thing. Almost nobody does all three consistently at the apprentice stage — not because they’re lazy, but because they never made the three a habit.

The Commitment Moves

  1. Say fewer yeses. One real yes beats three hopeful maybes.
  2. Write the promise down. If it’s not in Jira, a ticket, or a message, it doesn’t exist.
  3. Check your timeline daily. Not weekly. The slip is always detectable earlier than you admit.
  4. Raise the flag at 30% slip, not 100%. If you’re one-third over and you know it, say it. The later you flag, the worse it lands.
  5. Close the loop when done. “I finished X.” One line. It’s surprising how often this step is skipped.

A scenario: the quiet slip

⚠

On Monday you estimated two days. It’s Wednesday evening. You’re about half done. You don’t say anything — you’ll just push through Thursday. Thursday night you’re still at 70%. Friday standup: “almost done, should be by end of day.” Friday 6pm you push it to Monday. Monday morning: “there’s a bit more than I thought.” Tuesday it ships.

What actually happened: the slip was visible Wednesday. The cost of saying “I’m behind, looks like three days not two” on Wednesday is nothing. The cost of the silence is that nobody could re-plan around you, and by Friday you had burned the team’s trust as well as the timeline.

The commitment move: when you know, say. Before you know, estimate. After you finish, close the loop.

The overcommitment trap

The easiest way to break commitments is to accept too many. It feels like helpfulness. It lands as unreliability.

Common pitfall — Saying yes to everything

Someone asks you to pick up a thing. You say yes. Someone else asks. You say yes. Three days later you have six things on your plate and can’t meaningfully progress any of them.

Better — The honest capacity answer

“I’m on X and Y this week. I can pick this up after those land, or we can drop Y if this is more important.” You’re still helpful. You’re also still reliable.

Common pitfall — The hopeful yes

“Yeah, I can probably get that done by Friday.” Probably is not a commitment. Friday arrives and the probably didn’t.

Better — The specific yes or the specific no

“Yes, by Friday EOD.” Or: “Not by Friday — how about Tuesday?” A specific no now is worth a vague yes then.

Common pitfall — Abandoning what you started

You picked up a tech-debt task last sprint. You got 60% through. New story drops. You leave the old one half-done. Two sprints later nobody remembers the state, including you.

Better — Finish or hand off cleanly

If you’re truly moving on, write down what’s done, what’s not, and what the next person needs to know — and say out loud that you’re handing it off. A visible handover beats a silent abandonment every time.

Dos & Don’ts

DoDon’t
Commit to one thing fully before adding anotherCollect three concurrent “I’ll get to it”s
Re-estimate out loud when reality shiftsSilently hope it’ll still fit
Say “done” when it’s actually done, not when the last commit landedCall it done before you’ve run it yourself
Flag slips at 30%, not 100%Wait until the deadline to share bad news
Say “no” or “not this week” when it’s trueSay yes to be agreeable

Key Insight

Your reputation is built from the pile of small promises you did or didn’t keep. Not the one big heroic shipment. The boring, mid-sized, “by Wednesday” kind. Get those right for a year and people will trust you with the interesting work.

Further reading

  • The Missing README — Chris Riccomini & Dmitriy Ryaboy. Explicitly written for engineers at this career stage.
  • The Software Engineer’s Guidebook — Gergely Orosz. The chapter on estimation alone is worth it.
  • Deep Work — Cal Newport. On why shallow commitments crowd out deep work.
04 · The Foundational Habit

Propose, Don’t Just Complain

This is the single habit that, once built, compounds faster than any other. It’s also the single habit most missing at this career stage — and the one your team will notice first when you build it.

The pattern

Something is wrong. The build is slow. The onboarding is confusing. The logs are useless. Standups drift. A test is flaky. You notice it. That noticing is good — most people don’t even notice.

The trap is stopping there. Complaining about the problem — even accurately, even correctly — is the half-move. The full move is propose.

Key Insight

“Our builds are slow” is a complaint. “Our builds are slow; I looked and it’s the integration test stage — what if we ran those only on main?” is a proposal. The first gets a shrug. The second starts a conversation.

Why it matters more than it looks

Complaining without proposing trains the team to hear you as a complainer, not a problem-solver. Even when you’re right, people tune out. And more importantly, complaining keeps you passive — the problem becomes someone else’s to fix, which means it probably won’t get fixed.

Proposing flips the ownership. You’re no longer the person pointing; you’re the person solving. Even when the proposal is wrong, it moves the conversation forward — someone will correct it, and the problem inches toward resolution.

The PDC Move (Propose, Don’t Complain)

  1. Name the problem — specific, factual, one sentence. “Local startup takes 90 seconds.”
  2. Name the cost — why it matters. “Every context switch costs us a minute and a half.”
  3. Name a possible cause — your best current guess. “I think it’s the schema-migration step running every boot.”
  4. Propose one action — small, concrete, testable. “What if we cache the migration hash and skip when unchanged?”
  5. Offer to try — put your own hand up. “I can spike it tomorrow morning if useful.”

Scenario: the flaky test

🛠

Every few pipelines, OrderCheckoutIntegrationTest fails. People rerun it. Sometimes it fails twice. Nobody mentions it because everyone knows to rerun.

Complaint-only: in standup, “that flaky test is annoying again.” Murmurs of agreement. Nothing happens.

Proposal version: “Quick thing — OrderCheckoutIntegrationTest has flaked 4 of the last 15 runs. I looked at the fails — they all hit the same race around order-number generation. I think the test is assuming sequential IDs. I can try adding a synchronized block in the setup — 30-minute spike, happy to own it.”

The second version takes the same 30 seconds of standup time. It lands completely differently. You just became the person who fixes the flaky test.

When the proposal is wrong

It often will be. That’s fine. Your proposal is a starting point, not a finished answer. Someone more experienced will say “actually the race isn’t in ID generation, it’s in the inventory-lock timeout — but the right fix is similar.” You got educated, the team got a fix, and you stayed the person who owned the problem. That’s a good trade.

Common pitfall — Waiting for permission to propose

“I’m not senior enough — someone else should decide.” Nobody is coming. If you’re closest to the problem, you’re the right person to propose.

Better — Propose tentatively but propose

“This might be naive, but what about X?” leaves room for correction while still moving things forward. You don’t need certainty to propose — you just need to be thinking.

Common pitfall — The post-standup grumble

You bring up the frustration over lunch, in DMs, to one teammate. It goes nowhere because the people who can act don’t hear it.

Better — Raise it where decisions happen

Standup, retro, huddle, the pull request itself. The room where decisions happen is the room where your proposal has to go — even if it’s less comfortable there.

Common pitfall — Proposing without offering to help

“We should really fix X.” Okay. Who’s “we”? If you’re not volunteering, you’re delegating — and you’re not yet in a position to delegate.

Better — “I can try it”

Raise your own hand. It’s the move that separates a contributor from an onlooker. Even a small offer (“I can spike this”) changes the conversation.

🌱

Why this compounds: every proposal you make, even a bad one, is a reputation deposit. You become the person who does things. Six months of consistent proposing changes how the team sees you more than any single feature you shipped.

05 · Focus

Time & Focus

Writing code is a maker activity. It needs long, uninterrupted stretches. Your calendar, your chat apps, and your own habits all conspire against that. Defending your focus is a skill, and you have to learn it on purpose.

Why context switching is expensive

Every time you leave the problem — to check Slack, answer an email, hop into an unrelated huddle — you pay two costs: the time of the interruption, and the 10–20 minutes it takes to reload the problem into your head. Do that eight times a day and you’ve lost two hours before lunch. Not to meetings — to re-loading.

The Focus Moves

  1. Pick one deep slot per day. 90 minutes minimum. Same time, same rules — chat off, calendar blocked.
  2. Batch the shallow work. Check Slack, email, and PR reviews in two or three slots a day — not continuously.
  3. Leave yourself a breadcrumb. When you stop mid-task, write one sentence about where you were and what’s next. Reload time drops to seconds.
  4. Say no to optional meetings. If the agenda could be a doc and you wouldn’t present, ask if you need to be there.
  5. Respect other people’s focus. Before you ping someone, ask: is this urgent, or is it just on my mind? If not urgent, let it wait for their check-in slot.

Patterns & anti-patterns

Common pitfall — Slack as a second tab

Slack is open all day. Every notification pulls you out. You respond fast — people love it — but your own work is a patchwork of 15-minute fragments.

Better — Scheduled check-ins

Close Slack during your deep slot. Check it at 10:30, 1:30, and 4:30. Most “urgent” messages aren’t. The ones that are will find you.

Common pitfall — The midnight hero

You make up for a bad focus day by coding until midnight. You ship, but fragmented. Next day you’re slower, and the code you wrote half-asleep is the one that bugs will find.

Better — Protect tomorrow, not tonight

End on time. Plan tomorrow’s deep slot first thing tomorrow. Rested quality beats heroic hours almost every time.

🕐

Why this matters: engineers confuse being busy with being productive. Three focused hours of maker time will beat eight fragmented ones on almost any task. The fragmentation isn’t a fact of life — it’s the default you unlearn.

Further reading

  • Deep Work — Cal Newport. On why maker hours matter and how to defend them.
  • Paul Graham, “Maker’s Schedule, Manager’s Schedule.” Four pages. Read it once a year.
06 · Learning

The Exploration Habit

If every hour of your week goes into shipping tickets, you’re not growing — you’re maintaining. You need a small, consistent slice of time spent on learning, tinkering, and exploring outside the day’s work. One or two hours a week. Non-negotiable. Treat it like a meeting.

Why this isn’t optional

The tools, languages, and patterns you use three years from now don’t exist yet in your current codebase. If the only things you learn are the ones your current feature demands, your knowledge narrows over time. Exploration is how you keep your toolbox from shrinking.

The 1–2 Hour Rule

  1. Block 90 minutes on the calendar once a week. Recurring. Treat it as a team meeting — not optional.
  2. Pick one thing before the slot begins. “Read the tokio source that powers our async runtime.” “Try a small thing in a framework we don’t use.” “Rebuild our caching logic three different ways and see which I prefer.”
  3. Write one paragraph at the end. What did you learn? What surprised you? What would you propose next time you see a relevant problem?
  4. Share it once a month. Slack message, lunch-and-learn, an internal blog post. A learning you keep to yourself is half of a learning.

What “exploration” looks like

Read the source

The framework, library, or language runtime you use every day. Pick one file. Understand it. Repeat weekly.

Try an alternative

Your team uses X. The industry also has Y. Build a toy version of what you’re working on in Y. You’ll understand X better by contrast.

Do the post-mortem

A bug took you three days last month. Go back. Understand exactly why. Write it up. Look for the class of bug — you’ll recognise it next time.

Read one chapter

A book from the Resources page. Not the whole book. One chapter. Apply one thing from it this week.

Watch one talk

30–50 minutes of a conference talk from someone you respect. Take two notes. Try one idea.

Prototype a proposal

A proposal you made but never got picked up — build a small working version of it. Show it. “Here’s what I meant.”

🔮

Why this matters: senior engineers didn’t become senior because they read more books at night. They became senior because they kept a small, consistent exploration habit for years. A 90-minute weekly slot, applied for five years, is 390 hours of learning. That’s a graduate degree.

07 · Growth

Owning Your Own Growth

Your manager is not responsible for your career. They’re responsible for supporting it when you bring it to them. The initiative is yours — and so is the accountability. Waiting for the next performance cycle to find out how you’re doing is too slow.

The three levers you control

The Growth Triangle

  1. Goals. Know what you’re trying to build this quarter. Two specific things — not “get better at coding.” “Be able to design a small REST service end-to-end without asking for help on structure.” That’s a goal.
  2. Feedback. Ask for it. Specifically, frequently, from more than one person. Don’t wait for reviews.
  3. Reflection. Weekly or monthly. What worked. What didn’t. What pattern do you keep falling into?

Asking for feedback — the questions that actually work

“How am I doing?” is a useless question. It invites “you’re doing great.” Instead:

  • “What’s one thing you’ve seen me do well in the last month?” (specific, anchored in evidence)
  • “What’s one thing you’d want me to work on next?” (specific, forward-looking, asks for one thing not a list)
  • “If you were reviewing my MRs, what pattern would you notice me repeating?” (invites the small, compound feedback)
  • “Is there a habit I’m missing that would unlock something for me?” (opens up gaps you can’t see)
🎯

Ask once a month. From peers, not just your lead. Write down what you hear. Look for patterns across voices — if three people tell you the same thing, it’s not opinion, it’s signal.

Receiving feedback without defence

The hardest part. When someone tells you something you did wasn’t great, every instinct says “explain, defend, contextualise.” That instinct kills the feedback loop — people stop telling you things because it’s tiring.

The 24-Hour Rule

  1. In the moment: say “thank you — let me sit with that.” Nothing else.
  2. Within 24 hours: think about it. Is it fair? What was the actual behaviour that prompted it? What’s the smallest change that addresses it?
  3. Within a week: come back. “I thought about what you said. Here’s what I’m trying differently.” The loop closes.
Common pitfall — The immediate defence

“Yeah, but that was because…” Within three seconds of feedback landing, you’re explaining why it’s not a fair read. The other person learns: don’t bother giving this feedback again.

Better — The pause, then the return

Take the feedback. Think. Decide later whether you agree. Come back in a few days with either a changed behaviour or a respectful disagreement — but not in the first thirty seconds.

Common pitfall — Silent absorption

You take the feedback quietly, never respond, and never change. The giver has no idea if it landed. They stop giving.

Better — The visible return

Close the loop explicitly. “You flagged that my MRs were too large. I’ve been breaking them up. Let me know if they’re landing better.” This is the move that earns you more feedback next time.

Key Insight

Feedback is the fastest way to grow — and it has a price. The price is sitting with discomfort for 24 hours without arguing. Pay it. Everything else on this page is slower than the feedback loop you open when you stop being defensive.
