01 · Mindset & Career

Mindset & Career

The books that shape how you approach the job itself — not just the code. If you only read from one section, read from this one.

The Pragmatic Programmer

Andy Hunt & Dave Thomas. Foundational. Covers care, craft, and the attitude of a serious engineer. Read once now, again in two years.

The Missing README

Chris Riccomini & Dmitriy Ryaboy. Written explicitly for this career stage. Practical, unpretentious, high signal.

The Software Engineer’s Guidebook

Gergely Orosz. Career navigation through the first decade. The chapters on growth levels alone are worth it.

Thanks for the Feedback

Douglas Stone & Sheila Heen. The book on receiving feedback. Changes how you handle every review and 1:1.

Deep Work

Cal Newport. Why maker hours matter. How to protect them from the everything-is-a-notification modern workplace.

Radical Candor

Kim Scott. On direct, caring communication. IC chapters apply as much as manager ones.

The Effective Engineer

Edmond Lau. Focuses on leverage — the engineering choices that produce disproportionate impact. Short and practical.

Staff Engineer

Will Larson. Aspirational but valuable — interviews with staff engineers on what the work actually looks like. Read to see the shape of the path ahead.

The Staff Engineer’s Path

Tanya Reilly. The practical companion to Larson’s book. The chapters on “big-picture thinking” and “executing” apply well before the staff level.

02 · Clean Code & Craft

Clean Code & Craft

Reading, naming, designing, refactoring. The quiet practices that compound into a career.

Clean Code

Robert C. Martin. Controversial in parts — read with critical eye. But the chapters on naming, functions, and comments remain foundational.

A Philosophy of Software Design

John Ousterhout. Short, sharp, focused on managing complexity. One of the best modern books on design.

Refactoring (2nd ed.)

Martin Fowler. The catalogue of refactors and when to apply them. Reference for years to come.

Code Complete (2nd ed.)

Steve McConnell. The encyclopaedia. Dense but reliable. Skim first; return for specific topics.

Practical Object-Oriented Design

Sandi Metz. Ruby on the cover, universal in the lessons. Teaches you to think about messages and dependencies.

The Art of Readable Code

Boswell & Foucher. Short, focused on readability at the line and function level. Excellent for your stage.

03 · Debugging & Reading Code

Debugging & Reading Code

Every hour spent getting better at debugging saves ten somewhere else. These books teach the method.

Debugging

David Agans. Short, canonical. Nine rules that cover 95% of problems. Worth re-reading every year.

Working Effectively with Legacy Code

Michael Feathers. How to change code you didn’t write without breaking it. Applies to almost every real codebase.

Reading Legacy Code (online essays)

Various authors, notably Marianne Bellotti. Shorter reads on why old code is the way it is.

Site Reliability Engineering

Google SRE book, free online. The operations and reliability side. Chapters on incidents and postmortems are gold.

04 · Testing

Testing

From “I should probably write tests” to “tests are how I move fast without fear.”

Test-Driven Development: By Example

Kent Beck. The original. Short and practical. Read it, try it, decide for yourself.

Growing Object-Oriented Software, Guided by Tests

Freeman & Pryce. The deeper book. How tests shape design. Dense but rewarding.

Unit Testing Principles, Practices and Patterns

Vladimir Khorikov. A modern, opinionated take on what makes tests valuable vs brittle.

The Art of Unit Testing

Roy Osherove. A friendly introduction with real examples. Good starting point.

05 · Data & Systems

Data & Systems

For depth in databases, distributed systems, and the parts of the stack that bite in production.

Designing Data-Intensive Applications

Martin Kleppmann. The book on how real systems store and move data. The chapters on transactions and replication are career-changing.

Use the Index, Luke!

Markus Winand (use-the-index-luke.com). Free, focused, excellent on DB indexing. Short enough to finish in a weekend.

SQL Performance Explained

Markus Winand. Companion book to “Use the Index, Luke.” Deeper dive if the free site leaves you wanting more.

Accelerate

Forsgren, Humble, Kim. The research on what distinguishes high-performing engineering teams. Short; data-driven.

06 · Production, Security & Reliability

Production, Security & Reliability

When the dev team owns running the thing in production, this reading list is not optional. Pick the book that matches the part of production you touch most.

Site Reliability Engineering

Google SRE Book. Free online. The vocabulary of SLOs, error budgets, and incident response. The chapters on postmortems and on-call are canonical.

The Site Reliability Workbook

Google. The practical companion with case studies. Read alongside the SRE book.

Observability Engineering

Charity Majors, Liz Fong-Jones, George Miranda. The modern reference on logs, metrics, traces, and what comes after. Opinionated and sharp.

Accelerate

Forsgren, Humble, Kim. The data behind small-and-frequent deploys, trunk-based development, and what separates high-performing engineering teams. Listed here too because the shipping-safely chapter leans on it.

Continuous Delivery

Jez Humble & Dave Farley. The foundational text. Older now, but the principles still hold.

Database Reliability Engineering

Laine Campbell & Charity Majors. Deep on safe database operations — migrations, rollouts, incidents.

Web Security for Developers

Malcolm McDonald. Short, accessible, at the right depth for engineers who aren’t security specialists. Start here.

OWASP Top 10

owasp.org. Free. Refresh annually. You should be able to recognise each category in code.

Threat Modeling

Adam Shostack. The canonical book if you want depth. Start lighter; come here when you do.

The Tangled Web

Michal Zalewski. Deep, exceptional on browser security. A slow, rewarding read.

07 · AI Coding Tools

AI Coding Tools

A moving target. Books are too slow for this space — blogs and docs are the canon. These are the writers who’ve been the most honest and useful on what AI tools actually do.

Simon Willison’s blog

simonwillison.net. The clearest, most ongoing writeup of what AI tools can and can’t do. Updated weekly. Highly practical.

Addy Osmani on AI-assisted engineering

addyosmani.com. Practical writing on habits, workflows, and failure modes for engineers using AI coding tools.

Anthropic — Building Effective Agents

anthropic.com. Worth reading once to understand how these tools actually construct answers. Shapes how you prompt them.

GitHub Copilot / Cursor / Claude Code docs

Read the docs of the tool you use. The best-practice sections are usually lightly hidden and genuinely useful.

08 · Collaboration & Writing

Collaboration & Writing

When the dev team has no PM, BA, or separate QA, engineers become the talkers and the writers. These are the books for that side of the job.

Shape Up

Ryan Singer, Basecamp. Free online. How a small team ships without a PM buffer. The shaping model is useful on any team that owns its own scope.

The Mom Test

Rob Fitzpatrick. Short, practical book on how to talk to users without getting misleading feedback. Applies whenever you talk to anyone about a product.

Writing for Developers

Piotr Sarna & Cynthia Dunlop. The mechanics of good technical writing — docs, blogs, ADRs, READMEs.

Working in Public

Nadia Asparouhova. Collaboration patterns in open-source apply to any team with few formal role boundaries.

The Culture Map

Erin Meyer. Cross-cultural collaboration. Valuable on any distributed team.

Crucial Conversations

Patterson, Grenny, McMillan, Switzler. A handbook for the disagreements that matter. Read the chapter on “state your path” a few times.

Team Topologies

Matthew Skelton & Manuel Pais. On how teams are shaped and how they interact. Useful framing for teams that own everything end-to-end.

09 · Talks

Talks Worth Watching

A good talk can shape how you see a problem for years. These are on YouTube, free, and reward re-watching.

Kevlin Henney — “Seven Ineffective Coding Habits of Many Programmers”

Funny, wise, specific. Changes how you look at code.

Sandi Metz — “All the Little Things”

On finding and fixing the small design issues that compound. A masterclass in careful thinking.

Dan North — “Patterns of Effective Delivery”

A counter-intuitive, thoughtful talk on how good engineers actually work.

Kent Beck — “Software Design Is Human Relationships”

The later-career Beck is philosophical and worth sitting with.

Rich Hickey — “Simple Made Easy”

Clojure on the surface, timeless on design underneath. One of the best talks in the industry.

Gary Bernhardt — “Boundaries”

Functional core, imperative shell. Short. Will change how you structure applications.

10 · Blogs & Newsletters

Blogs & Newsletters

Pick two or three. Subscribe. Actually read them.

Martin Fowler

martinfowler.com. Short, focused pieces on design, refactoring, testing, architecture. Reference-quality.

Julia Evans

jvns.ca. A masterclass in publicly practising curiosity. Her zines on debugging, containers, Linux, and git are excellent.

Dan Luu

danluu.com. Long, deeply-researched pieces. Slower reading, higher signal. Try “Diseconomies of scale” or “Normalization of deviance.”

The Pragmatic Engineer

Gergely Orosz’s newsletter. Industry news and career-focused writing for engineers at this stage and above.

Charity Majors

charity.wtf. Opinionated, sharp writing on observability, on-call, and the operational side of software. About ten years ahead of most teams.

Will Larson (lethain.com)

lethain.com. The writing that became Staff Engineer and An Elegant Puzzle. Practical and direction-setting.

Simon Willison

simonwillison.net. The best ongoing writeup of AI coding tools, plus strong essays on SQLite, Django, and building software in public.

Increment

increment.com. Long-form engineering writing organised by theme. Browse the back issues.

Lara Hogan

larahogan.me. On management and IC growth — the feedback, resilience, and communication writing applies to any engineer.

Brendan Gregg

brendangregg.com. Performance at depth. Essential when you need to go deep on systems-level perf.

11 · Podcasts

Podcasts

Put one in your ears on a walk, a commute, or while you wash dishes. The ones below are the ones worth staying subscribed to.

The Pragmatic Engineer Podcast

Gergely Orosz. Interviews with engineers and engineering leaders about how software actually gets built in different companies. High signal.

The Changelog

changelog.com. Long-running, wide-ranging conversations with developers behind open-source and industry trends. Friendly tone, serious guests.

Software Engineering Daily

softwareengineeringdaily.com. Daily interviews across the stack. Good for grazing; skip what doesn’t match your current work.

CoRecursive

corecursive.com. Stories behind software. Quieter, more narrative. Episodes like “The Perfect Bug Report” are classics.

Thoughtworks Technology Podcast

thoughtworks.com/insights/podcasts. Discussions of architectural and delivery patterns from the team behind the Technology Radar.

Oxide and Friends

oxide.computer/podcasts. Long-form technical conversations between senior engineers. Weekly. Excellent when you’re ready to dig deep.

Hanselminutes

hanselminutes.com. Scott Hanselman interviews people shaping software and the web. Broad, human, consistently worth your 30 minutes.

The Stack Overflow Podcast

stackoverflow.blog/podcast. Conversations about developer life and craft from the Stack Overflow team and guests.

12 · Practice

How to Actually Use These

The failure mode with a list like this is to bookmark everything and read nothing. A small habit beats a huge backlog.

The Learning Loop

  1. Pick one. One book or one talk. Not five.
  2. Finish it, even if you’re 50% bored by chapter three. Skim the boring parts; don’t abandon.
  3. Extract one idea. What’s the one thing you’ll try differently at work?
  4. Apply it this week. On real code, in a real MR.
  5. Share what you learned. A Slack message, a lunch-and-learn, a team wiki entry. Teaching solidifies the learning.
  6. Pick the next one. Move on. Don’t stockpile.
🌱

Why this matters: reading ten books and applying none of them is the same as reading none of them. Reading two books and applying one thing from each changes your career. The delta between the two is the difference between knowledge and skill.

The stage-sequencing suggestion

If you’re looking for a path through this list over the next year:

  1. Start with The Pragmatic Programmer or The Missing README. The mindset books ground everything else.
  2. Then one craft book (A Philosophy of Software Design or Refactoring).
  3. Then one debugging/testing book (Debugging by Agans, or Growing Object-Oriented Software).
  4. Then one systems book (Designing Data-Intensive Applications if you touch backend; skip if you don’t).
  5. Sprinkle talks and blog posts throughout. They’re shorter and often set you up for the next book.

Four books in a year, actually finished and actually applied, is a lot. Don’t try for more.

Knowledge without practice is nothing; practice without knowledge is dangerous.

— Confucius (approximately), and every good engineering mentor
