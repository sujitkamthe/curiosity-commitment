01 · Pairing

Pairing Etiquette

Pairing is one of the fastest ways to learn and one of the fastest ways to ship hard problems — if both people are actually present. Done badly, it’s two people half-watching one person code. Done well, it’s more than twice as effective as solo work.

Roles — driver and navigator

In a good pairing session, one person is driving (typing) and the other is navigating (thinking about the bigger picture, catching mistakes, suggesting approaches). These are different cognitive modes. You can’t do both well at the same time.

The Pairing Moves

  1. Switch every 10–20 minutes. Either person can call the switch. Both stay engaged because both are about to take the keyboard.
  2. Driver thinks out loud. “I’m about to rename this to X…” Narration lets the navigator catch mistakes early.
  3. Navigator stays one step ahead. Don’t watch the driver type — think about what happens after the current block.
  4. Agree on the goal before starting. “In the next 90 minutes we’ll get the failing test to pass and refactor X.” Without this, you drift.
  5. Take breaks. Pairing is intense. 90 minutes, then a proper break.

What good pairing looks like

  • Both people engaged; neither person silent or zoned out
  • Keyboard switches fluidly; no “who’s driving” confusion
  • Disagreements are explored out loud, not avoided
  • The quieter person gets pulled in (“what do you think?”)
  • Progress you couldn’t have made alone, and both people learned something
Common pitfall — Navigator on their phone

You’re physically there but not actually watching. Driver makes a mistake. You miss it. The “pair” was solo with an audience.

Better — Present or not there at all

If you’re not going to pair, say so — “I need to step out”. A 30-minute focused pair beats 2 hours of half-presence.

Common pitfall — Silent driver

You type without narrating. The navigator doesn’t know what you’re about to do. Catches happen after the fact instead of before.

Better — Narrate intent

“I’m going to extract this into a function called X because Y.” Navigator can course-correct cheaply.

Common pitfall — One person drives for 3 hours

The navigator fades. The driver is also tired. Quality drops on both sides.

Better — Switch regularly

10–20 minutes. If you’re tracking in Pomodoros, switch each break. The switch refreshes both minds.

💡

Why this matters: pairing is expensive (two people, one keyboard). It’s worth it when the work is genuinely hard, when you’re teaching or learning, or when the cost of a mistake is high (tricky migration, security-sensitive code). For routine work, solo is usually fine. Know which is which.

02 · Standups

Standups That Matter

Most standups drift into a status-reading exercise that nobody looks forward to. Your job at this stage isn’t to fix that alone — but your contributions to it can help reset what the meeting is for. Use the time to coordinate, not to report.

What standup is actually for

Standup exists for three reasons: align on who’s working on what, surface blockers, and offer or ask for help. It is not a status report to anyone (that happens in 1:1s). If the meeting has drifted to status reporting, your individual update can still serve the real goal — but the content changes.

A Useful Standup Update

  1. What I shipped since yesterday — one line. “Yesterday the pricing refactor merged.”
  2. What I’m on today — one line. “Today I’m debugging the timeout on the checkout API.”
  3. Where I’m stuck (if anywhere) — specific and brief. “I’m blocked on the test env — it’s been down since yesterday.”
  4. Help I need or can offer — if relevant. “If anyone’s familiar with the payment-service circuit breaker, I’d love 15 min.”

The art of not talking in standup

Not every item deserves airtime. If your debug conversation is going to take 10 minutes, take it out of standup — “let’s talk after, I have specific questions.” The group doesn’t need to watch two people work through it.

Common pitfall — The vague update

“Yesterday I was working on the order stuff. Today I’ll continue on that.” Three days in a row. Nobody knows if you’re making progress.

Better — Specific and shippable

“Finished the pricing logic, MR up. Today I’m on the webhook retries — aiming to have a draft by EOD.” Progress is visible.

Common pitfall — Hiding blockers

You say “things are fine” because admitting you’re stuck feels bad. Two days later you’re still stuck and now the team is behind.

Better — Name the stuck, ask for help

“Stuck on X since yesterday. I’ve tried A and B. Anyone seen this?” Usually someone has, 30 seconds, done. The “stuck is embarrassing” instinct is one of the most expensive ones in a team.

Common pitfall — Detailed technical discussion in standup

Two people debug a problem in real-time while eight people listen.

Better — Table it

“Let’s take this after standup.” Group meeting; two-person conversation. Keep them separate.

🎯

Your contribution to standup culture: model the format. Be concise, specific, and honest about blockers. Ask your specific question or offer your specific help. When a couple of people consistently do this, standup slowly becomes useful.

03 · Onboarding

Helping Newer Joiners

Every time someone new joins, the team has a choice: make their first weeks painful or make them useful. The choice is mostly made by whoever happens to be around, willing to spend 15 minutes. That person can be you.

Why this is part of your job

You probably just joined a team yourself a year or two ago. Remember how disorienting the first weeks were? The random meetings that assumed context, the documentation that was three years out of date, the senior engineer who was “happy to help” but too busy to actually respond. You’re now the person who can fix that for the next joiner.

Key Insight

Helping a newer joiner is not charity. It’s one of the highest-leverage things you can do for the team. A joiner who ramps up in 3 weeks instead of 8 saves you and everyone else months of explaining over time.

What “helping” actually looks like

First-day walk-through

Thirty minutes. Take them through the main flow of the product as a user, then the main flow in code. Answer the obvious questions they’re too new to ask.

Be their first DM-friendly person

“DM me anything in the first two weeks, nothing is too basic.” Remove the friction of asking.

Pair on their first ticket

An hour of pairing on their first real task teaches more than a week of solo ramp-up.

Review their first MR carefully — and kindly

The tone of their first review shapes how they show up in reviews for months. Be direct about quality; be warm about people.

Update docs as they hit gaps

They hit a confusion. You explain. Now you add that explanation to the README. The next joiner finds it there.

Introduce them to people

“If you need X, talk to Priya.” The social network of the team is non-obvious. Help them build it.

The “silly question” pledge

Make it safe for them to ask anything. “There are no silly questions” is a cliché; “I’ll answer any question for two weeks, no judgement” is a commitment. The latter lands. Keep it.

Common pitfall — “Ask me anytime” that’s not real

You said it but you’re always heads-down. They DM; you respond two days later. They stop asking.

Better — Scheduled check-ins for the first two weeks

15 minutes every day for the first week; a couple of times in the second. They save questions; you plow through a batch. Both of you get time back.

Common pitfall — Leaving onboarding to “the leader”

You assume the team lead is handling it. They’re handling the strategic parts; the day-to-day unblocking is on the team.

Better — Claim a piece

“I’ll walk them through the order service on day 3.” Specific, owned, one hour. Repeats across three teammates and the new person has a real ramp-up plan.

04 · Async

Async Written Communication

In a distributed or partly-distributed team, most of your work-related communication is written. Slack, email, PR comments, tickets. Writing well here is not a literary skill — it’s a productivity skill. Good writing respects the reader’s time; bad writing wastes it in ways that compound.

The core principle: respect the reader’s time

Every message you write will be read by N people, where N is often more than you think. Five extra seconds of clarity in the writing saves five seconds × N × every future reader. This arithmetic always favours the writer spending the extra time.

The Async Writing Moves

  1. Lead with the conclusion. “We need to roll back. Why follows.” Not “So, I was looking at…”
  2. One point per message. Don’t stack “also… and another thing…”
  3. Be specific. File paths, dates, names, numbers. Not “the thing we talked about.”
  4. Say what you want. An answer? A decision? Acknowledgement? Say so.
  5. Say the urgency. “Blocking me today” vs “whenever” vs “for context, no response needed.”
  6. Use threads for detail. Keep the channel’s scannable surface clean.

Scenario: two Slack messages

🛠

Message A (wasteful): “Hey, so I was looking at the checkout thing and I noticed some things and I think we might need to do something about it. Got a minute?”

Message B (respectful): “Heads up — checkout latency p95 has doubled since yesterday’s deploy (600ms → 1.2s). I think it’s the new inventory lookup. I’m looking now. Will flag in #incidents if it looks worse than a minor regression. (No action needed from you unless I escalate.)”

Message A is a 5-minute conversation waiting to happen. Message B conveys everything in 30 seconds of reading and keeps the recipient unblocked.

Public over private — mostly

Default to public channels over DMs. Three reasons:

  1. Someone else might have the answer faster.
  2. The answer becomes searchable for the next person.
  3. You don’t create a single-person dependency.

Exceptions: anything sensitive, people-adjacent, or about a mistake you made. Those stay private.

Common pitfall — “Hi!” then waiting

You DM “Hi!” and wait for them to reply before asking the question. That’s a round-trip you could have saved.

Better — Lead with the question

“Hey — quick Q when you’re free: do we have a rate limit on the external API? I’m hitting 429s.” They can answer whenever. No round trip.

Common pitfall — The wall of text

A 400-word message with no structure. Reader’s eyes glaze. They either skim and miss the ask, or put it off for later.

Better — TL;DR at the top, detail below

“TL;DR: need a decision on X by Thursday. Detail follows.” Reader knows the ask in ten seconds; can read detail if they need it.

Common pitfall — Confusing channel with DM

You ask a technical question in DMs. Two days later a teammate hits the same thing and you explain it again.

Better — Team channels for team questions

The answer gets indexed, teammates benefit. Cost: basically zero.

05 · Citizenship

Givers, Takers, and the Compound Cost

Every team has three groups: people who give more than they take, people who give and take roughly evenly, and people who mostly take. You don’t need a badge or a title to be in the first group — you just need the habit. And over time, the habit defines your reputation more than any feature you shipped.

What giving actually looks like

It’s small. Unglamorous. Un-career-accelerating in any single instance. But it compounds. It’s:

  • Reviewing a peer’s MR within hours instead of days
  • Answering a question in #general when you could have left it
  • Fixing the broken setup script after you hit it, instead of working around it
  • Updating the README when the steps you followed were out of date
  • Helping a teammate unblock themselves instead of doing it for them
  • Staying ten minutes after a meeting to clarify with the person who didn’t follow
  • Taking the unglamorous ticket because someone has to
  • Mentoring without being asked to mentor

Key Insight

Almost none of these show up in a performance review. All of them are visible to peers. A year of giving at this level builds you a reputation no review can: the engineer people actually want to work with.

What taking looks like (and why to notice it in yourself)

Taking isn’t malicious. Most of the time it’s a lapse of attention: you’re busy, you’re stressed, and you don’t think about the costs your pattern imposes on others.

  • Leaving MRs to rot because “I’m not really on this team for reviews”
  • Asking questions in DMs that could have been searched first
  • Handing broken scripts to the next person
  • Pinging people at 10pm for non-urgent things
  • Hoarding knowledge because it makes you look indispensable
  • Never volunteering for the unglamorous ticket

Notice these in yourself. They’re not character flaws; they’re lapses. Correcting them is cheap. Not correcting them, over two years, costs you meaningfully.

🌱

The compounding: when you need a favour, a stretch ticket, a reference for your next job, or simply to be given the benefit of the doubt on a rough week — the people who answer are the people you’ve been giving to for months or years. Investment made quietly over time; yielded at precisely the moments you need it.

Where to start if you notice you’re a taker

Pick one small thing and do it weekly for a month:

  • Review two MRs a week that you weren’t tagged on
  • Answer one question in a team channel that you could have left
  • Fix one small annoyance in the dev setup that’s been bothering you
  • Update one page of docs that’s stale
  • Help one new joiner without being asked

The shift from taker to giver isn’t a conversion moment. It’s five or six small habits that, over a quarter, quietly change how your teammates feel about working with you.

06 · Process

Agile Practices — Not Rituals

Most teams have some version of agile: standups, sprint planning, retros, estimation. Most of it, most of the time, feels like ritual. Your job at this stage isn’t to critique the process — it’s to understand what each practice is trying to buy, and to contribute in a way that makes the practice actually deliver that.

What each practice is trying to achieve

PracticeWhat it’s actually forWhat it becomes when you don’t contribute
StandupCoordination, blocker-surfacing, help-offeringStatus-report recital
Sprint planningShared understanding of what’s getting done, who’s doing what, what the risks areLead assigning tickets while people nod
Backlog refinementStories are clear and estimable before you commit to themStories are committed to vague, then clarified mid-sprint
RetroSurfacing what went well and what to improve; committing to one thingA graveyard of “action items” nobody owns
DemosSeeing the real thing, catching misalignment with stakeholders earlyA slide deck someone made under duress
EstimationCollective calibration on size/risk; surface unknownsNumber-calling contest

Your contributions

Making Agile Work — From Your Seat

  1. Standup: be concise, raise blockers, offer help. Don’t use it for status theatre.
  2. Sprint planning: come prepared having read the stories. Ask “what’s the goal?” before “how long”.
  3. Refinement: read stories ahead. Ask the clarification questions. Flag when acceptance criteria are missing.
  4. Retro: bring one specific observation. Own one action item. Follow up on last retro’s actions.
  5. Demos: run your own feature. Narrate. Invite the specific feedback you want.
  6. Estimation: estimate honestly. Flag unknowns explicitly. Push for a spike if needed.
💡

The reframe: the ritual is fine. It’s just a container. What fills the container is what the team puts in it. If you show up passively, you make the container feel empty. If you show up prepared and contribute, the meeting earns its time. Your part is what you control.

Common pitfall — Complaining about agile without proposing

“These ceremonies are useless.” See Propose, don’t complain. The ceremony isn’t the problem; the way your team runs it is. Offer an alternative.

Better — Retro-time proposals

“Standup has been long. What if we try a two-minute-per-person cap for the next two weeks, then see?” Small, time-boxed, proposable.

Common pitfall — Ghosting retro actions

The retro produces action items. Nobody follows up. Next retro has the same items. The ritual becomes a cynical exercise.

Better — Own one action

Volunteer for one retro action. Do it. Report back next retro. Even one person following through changes the retro’s culture.

Further reading

  • The Scrum Guide (free, 13 pages) — the minimal definition of Scrum. Worth reading once so you know what the ritual is supposed to be.
  • Accelerate — Forsgren, Humble, Kim. The research on what high-performing teams actually do.
  • The Mythical Man-Month — Fred Brooks. Old. Still relevant. Why communication is the dominant cost on any team.

One thing to try this week

  • Switch who’s driving every 15 minutes in your next pair; narrate intent.
  • Volunteer to walk a newer joiner through one flow this week.
  • Review one MR you weren’t explicitly tagged on.
  • Lead your next standup update with what you’re blocked on, not just what you’re on.
  • Own one action item from the next retro through to completion.
